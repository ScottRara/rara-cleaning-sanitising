Proper Cleaning & Sanitising for Kitchen Staff

Learning Objectives: Understand the critical difference between cleaning and sanitising, master the 6-step cleaning procedure, know correct temperatures and chemical concentrations, follow proper handwashing technique, and implement daily/weekly cleaning schedules at RaRa Ramen.

1. Cleaning vs Sanitising — Know the Difference

Many kitchen staff use the words "cleaning" and "sanitising" interchangeably. They are not the same thing, and understanding the difference is fundamental to food safety.

Cleaning Sanitising
What it does: Removes visible dirt, food particles, grease, and grime from a surface What it does: Applies heat or chemicals to destroy or reduce microorganisms (bacteria) to a safe level
Uses: Hot water + detergent + physical scrubbing Uses: High-temperature water (77°C+) OR food-grade chemical sanitiser
Removes: Visible contamination, grease, food residue, allergens Removes: Invisible bacteria, pathogens, microorganisms
Without it: Surfaces look dirty, food residue builds up, attracts pests Without it: Bacteria multiply to dangerous levels, causing foodborne illness
Critical Rule Sanitising is ONLY effective on surfaces that have already been thoroughly cleaned. Wiping a sanitiser over an uncleaned surface just spreads the bacteria around. You must ALWAYS clean first, THEN sanitise.
Does sanitising replace cleaning?
NO. Sanitising only works on surfaces that are already clean. You must always clean first, then sanitise.
What does cleaning remove?
Visible dirt, food particles, grease, grime, and food allergens. It uses hot water, detergent, and physical scrubbing.
What does sanitising remove?
Invisible bacteria and microorganisms, reduced to a safe level using heat (77°C+) or approved chemical sanitisers.

2. The 6-Step Cleaning & Sanitising Procedure

Every surface, utensil, and piece of equipment at RaRa Ramen must be cleaned and sanitised following this strict six-step process. No shortcuts.

1️⃣
Step 1: Pre-Clean

Scrape or wipe food scraps and other matter off surfaces. Rinse with water to remove loose debris. This step prevents food waste from contaminating your wash water and reduces the load on detergent.

2️⃣
Step 2: Wash

Use hot water and detergent to remove grease and food residue. Soak items if necessary. The physical scrubbing action is critical — it's the friction that removes contamination. Wash in hot soapy water at 45°C.

3️⃣
Step 3: Rinse

Rinse off the detergent and any loosened dirt or residue with clean water. Detergent residue left on surfaces can contaminate food and reduce the effectiveness of sanitisers.

4️⃣
Step 4: Sanitise

Apply a food-grade sanitiser or use heat to destroy remaining microorganisms. Allow the appropriate contact time as specified by the manufacturer's instructions. Do not rush this step.

5️⃣
Step 5: Final Rinse

Wash off the chemical sanitiser if the manufacturer's instructions require it. Some sanitisers are designed to be left on (no-rinse), others must be rinsed. Always check the label.

6️⃣
Step 6: Air Dry

Allow utensils, crockery, and benches to air dry, or use single-use paper towels. Never use tea towels or shared cloths to dry — they spread bacteria. Air drying is important because bacteria transfer much more easily onto wet surfaces.

Clean As You Go Don't wait until the end of service. Adopt a "clean as you go" approach throughout your shift. Equipment in constant contact with potentially hazardous foods at room temperature (e.g., meat slicers, blenders, stick mixers) must be cleaned and sanitised at least every 4 hours.

3. Temperatures & Chemical Sanitisers

There are two methods of sanitising: heat (thermal) and chemical. Both are effective when used correctly.

Thermal Sanitising (Heat)

MethodWash TemperatureSanitise TemperatureContact Time
Hand washing dishes/appliances 45°C 77°C At least 30 seconds
Commercial dishwasher — wash cycle 66–71°C 60 seconds
Commercial dishwasher — rinse/sanitise cycle 82°C 10 seconds
Thermometer probes 77°C 30 seconds
No Economy Cycles Never use the "economy" or "eco" setting on commercial dishwashers. These cycles do not reach high enough temperatures to properly sanitise. Always use the hottest wash and rinse cycles available.
Safety: Wear Thick Gloves When hand-sanitising at 77°C, you MUST wear thick rubber gloves to avoid burns. This water is extremely hot.

Chemical Sanitising

When high temperatures cannot be achieved, approved food-grade chemical sanitisers must be used. These are typically chlorine-based (commercial bleach) or ammonium-based compounds designed for food premises.

Bleach Dilution Guide (10% Chlorine Commercial Bleach)

Dilution rate:1 mL of bleach in 1 Litre of cold water
Contact time:Minimum 7 seconds on the surface
Application:Spray bottle, sink immersion, or dishwasher final rinse
Important:Use cold water — hot water can cause chlorine to evaporate and become less effective

Chemical Safety Rules

  • Always read the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) and manufacturer's instructions for every chemical product
  • Never mix different chemicals together
  • Always follow the correct dilution rates — too little won't sanitise, too much can contaminate food
  • Always allow the correct contact time before rinsing (if required)
  • Store chemicals in their original labelled containers, away from food and food preparation areas
  • Use appropriate PPE (gloves, eye protection) as directed on the SDS

4. Handwashing — Your #1 Defence

Proper handwashing is the single most important thing you can do to prevent foodborne illness. Hands must always be washed in a dedicated handwashing basin — never in sinks used for food preparation or utensil washing.

The Correct Handwashing Steps

1
Wet hands under warm running water
2
Apply soap (liquid soap from a dispenser — not bar soap)
3
Scrub vigorously for 15–30 seconds — palms, between fingers, under nails, backs of hands, thumbs, and wrists. Use a clean nail brush for tough food residues.
4
Rinse thoroughly under clean running water
5
Turn off the tap without re-contaminating your hands (use a paper towel or elbow)
6
Dry completely using a single-use paper towel. Drying is just as important as washing — bacteria transfer much more easily onto wet hands.
What Makes It Effective
  • Friction & time (15–30 seconds of scrubbing) is the most critical factor — more important than water temperature
  • Soap is necessary to remove grease, dirt, and microorganisms. Liquid dispensers are preferred over bar soap.
  • Hand sanitiser is NOT a substitute for washing with soap and water
  • Tea towels and aprons must NEVER be used for drying hands — they actively cross-contaminate

5. Glove Usage

Gloves Are NOT a Substitute for Clean Hands Hands must ALWAYS be washed thoroughly before putting on gloves and between glove changes. Gloves are single-use only and must never be washed and reused.

When to Wear Gloves

  • When handling ready-to-eat foods (salads, garnishes, plated food)
  • When handling raw meat, poultry, or seafood
  • When you have a cut or wound (which must also be covered by a brightly coloured, waterproof dressing beneath the glove)

When to Change Gloves

  • When switching between raw and ready-to-eat foods
  • After touching your face, hair, or phone
  • After handling money
  • After cleaning or taking out trash
  • After any activity where gloves may have picked up bacteria
Allergy & Safety Considerations
  • Always use latex-free gloves to protect customers with latex allergies
  • Check that non-latex gloves do not contain colloidal oatmeal (contains gluten — can trigger reactions in people with wheat allergy or coeliac disease)
  • Use different coloured gloves for different areas (e.g., bathrooms vs kitchen) to prevent cross-contamination between zones

6. Cross-Contamination Prevention Through Cleaning

Cross-contamination is the transfer of harmful bacteria or allergens from one surface to another. Proper cleaning is your primary defence.

Colour-Coded Chopping Board System

ColourUse
RedRaw meat
BlueRaw fish / seafood
YellowRaw poultry
GreenFruits & vegetables
WhiteBread / bakery / dairy
BrownCooked meat

Key Cross-Contamination Rules

  • Clean and sanitise food contact surfaces, cutting boards, and knives between uses — especially when switching between raw and ready-to-eat foods
  • Thermometer probes must be wiped, washed, and sanitised (alcohol swabs or hot water at 77°C for 30 seconds) before and after every use
  • Wiping cloths must not be used as multipurpose tools. Replace frequently, or wash and soak in sanitiser daily. Use colour-coded cloths for different areas.
  • Damaged utensils (chipped, cracked, or broken) must be disposed of immediately — they cannot be effectively sanitised and can harbour bacteria in crevices or cause physical contamination
  • Store raw foods (especially meat and poultry) on bottom shelves in the fridge, securely wrapped, to prevent drips onto ready-to-eat food below
  • Wash hands before wiping down benches — using a cloth with unwashed hands transfers bacteria directly onto food surfaces

7. Cleaning Schedules

RaRa Ramen must maintain strict daily and weekly cleaning schedules to ensure no area or piece of equipment is missed.

Daily Cleaning Tasks

  • All food prep areas, surfaces, cutting boards & utensils — cleaned and sanitised
  • Cooking equipment (ovens, grills, fryers, stovetops) — cleaned to prevent fire hazards and grease buildup
  • Slicers, blenders, and stick mixers — cleaned and sanitised (at least every 4 hours during use)
  • Floors mopped and drains cleared
  • Garbage bins emptied, cleaned, and sanitised
  • Handwash stations restocked (soap, paper towels)
  • Wiping cloths replaced or washed and soaked in sanitiser
  • Thermometer probes cleaned and sanitised

Weekly Cleaning Tasks

  • Fridges and cool rooms emptied and cleaned
  • Food waste containers thoroughly cleaned
  • Shelving and store areas cleaned
  • Behind and under equipment
  • Exhaust fans and rangehood filters
  • Walls and splash-backs in prep areas
  • Deep clean of drains
  • Review and update cleaning register
Cleaning & Sanitising Register The Food Safety Supervisor oversees a cleaning register that documents exactly when specific areas or equipment were cleaned, and who completed the task. This is a legal requirement and may be inspected during audits.

8. Waste Management & Pest Prevention

Garbage Rules

  • Use bins with tight-fitting lids and bin liners — lids must stay closed during use
  • Bins must never overflow — remove garbage regularly
  • Keep bins away from food preparation areas
  • Clean and sanitise bins daily: remove liners, scrub inside and out with hot water and detergent, rinse, soak with sanitiser, air dry upside down, then replace liner
  • Never use garbage containers to transfer food or ice
  • Always wash hands immediately after handling garbage
Golden Rule for Food Waste "If in doubt, throw it out!" — Any food that is recalled, returned, or suspected of contamination must be clearly marked, kept separate from fresh food, and disposed of promptly.

Pest Prevention

Under the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code (Standard 3.2.2), food businesses must take all practicable measures to prevent pests and eradicate them from the premises.

  • Block and seal holes, cracks, and gaps in walls and floors
  • Use fly screens on doors and windows
  • Keep food off the floor in airtight containers
  • Never leave food out overnight
  • Never use regular fly sprays in food preparation or service areas — chemical droplets contaminate food
  • Use blue light zappers, baits, and traps away from food
  • Use a licensed pest control company for regular treatments
  • Drains must never be left uncovered — open drains are a primary entry point for rats and cockroaches

9. Food Safety Culture at RaRa Ramen

A strong food safety culture means that food safety is treated as a top priority — not just a checklist to complete. It starts with the right attitudes and behaviours from every team member.

What Good Food Safety Culture Looks Like

  • Every team member understands why cleaning procedures matter, not just what to do
  • If an error occurs, staff ensure potentially unsafe food is never served
  • Staff speak up about food safety concerns without fear
  • The "clean as you go" approach is second nature, not an afterthought
  • Records and registers are kept honestly and accurately

The Food Safety Supervisor's Role

  • Monitors cleaning procedures through routine visual inspections and audits
  • Maintains cleaning and sanitising registers
  • Takes corrective action when failures are identified (improper sanitiser use, missed cleaning, pest signs)
  • Trains staff in proper hygiene and cleaning procedures
  • Reviews and updates the food safety manual and cleaning schedules regularly
